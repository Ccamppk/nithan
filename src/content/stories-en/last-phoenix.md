Saeng walked through the forest behind school every afternoon. Same path, same trees — but there was always something new to find.

Today he found a small bird lying at the base of a large tree. Roughly the size of his fist, with pale orange-red feathers and a left wing bent at the wrong angle.

He crouched down. The bird looked up at him with tired golden eyes.

"Who hurt you?" he said quietly, then gently lifted it in both hands. It was warm and impossibly light.

He carried it home, found a cardboard box, lined it with old cloth, and gave it water and soft rice a little at a time. He made a splint from ice-cream sticks and medical tape, the way he'd seen in a library book.

His mother came to look. "What kind of bird is that? The color is unusual."

"I don't know yet," Saeng said. "But I'll take care of it first."

He named it Fire.

---

Fire recovered slowly. It ate more, and its faded feathers deepened to vivid orange and red and gold — colors Saeng had never seen on any bird before.

But one thing was strange. Fire never called out. Never fluttered its wings. And the air around it was always cool, as though something inside had been extinguished.

One evening while Saeng did homework beside the box, Fire climbed to the edge and sat watching him.

"Why don't you ever make a sound?" Saeng asked.

Fire said nothing.

Saeng opened his encyclopedia of animals and searched through every orange-red bird until he stopped on one page.

*Phoenix. Fire-bird of legend. Orange-red-gold feathers. Lives for thousands of years. Can rise from its own ashes. Believed to be extinct.*

He looked at Fire. Fire looked at him.

"Are you... a phoenix?"

Fire lifted its wings — just slightly — the first time it had ever moved them.

---

Saeng read further. The fire of a phoenix, the book said, didn't come from magic. It came from love and belief. When no one believed the phoenix existed anymore, its fire would slowly go out.

He set the book down and looked at Fire, sitting small and still in its box.

"I believe you," he said quietly and directly. "I believe you're really a phoenix. Not just a regular bird."

Fire watched him.

"And I'm glad you're here. Whatever you are."

Nothing happened. Saeng sighed and leaned close to whisper very softly: "I love you, Fire."

And then warmth spread from the bird's body, sudden and real. A faint orange-gold light flickered at the tips of its feathers and grew brighter and brighter until the whole room was filled with a gentle, sun-like glow.

Fire raised its head and called out — the first sound it had ever made. A sound that made Saeng want to cry without knowing why.

His mother rushed in. She stopped in the doorway, staring at the glowing bird on the cardboard box.

"Saeng. What is that?"

"A phoenix," he said softly, his chest full. "The last one."

That night, Saeng lay watching Fire glow warm and steady on its perch. Fire wasn't a cage bird — it was a friend who had chosen to stay.

And each night after that, its light grew a little brighter. Because someone believed in it, and loved it.
