Dang was a mixed-breed dog, white with black spots, just about the size of a child's lap.

He lived with Nan's family, a ten-year-old girl, in a house with a backyard just big enough to run around in — but fenced in on every side.

Dang loved lying by the fence, gazing out at the world beyond. He could see the street, the tall trees, and stray cats wandering freely wherever they pleased.

I want to go exploring too, Dang thought to himself, every single day.

One day, a repairman came to fix the fence and left a small gap while he took a lunch break. Dang stared at the gap for a moment, then slipped quietly through.

The world outside was bigger than he ever imagined.

Hundreds of new smells rushed past his nose. The sound of cars, of people, of birds in the trees. Dang stood in front of the house for a moment, his heart racing with excitement, then began walking down the street.

He passed a small market filled with the smell of delicious food, a park where children ran and played, a small canal where ducks swam by.

Everything was thrilling and beautiful.

But as time passed, Dang began to realize he no longer knew which way he had come from. Every street looked the same.

The sky began to darken.

Dang tried running this way, then that way, but the more he ran, the more lost he became. His stomach growled with hunger, and the air grew colder and colder.

He sat down at the edge of the road, ears drooping, tail low, feeling afraid for the first time since he'd left home.

Then he heard a familiar sound.

"Dang!"

It was Nan's voice. She came running with her mother and father, all three of them carrying flashlights, their faces full of worry.

Dang ran straight to Nan, jumping up to lick her face again and again. Nan scooped him up tightly and cried softly.

"I was so scared. Where did you go, Dang?" she said, her voice trembling.

Dang buried his face in her chest, feeling warm and safe.

Back home, Nan brought him rice to eat, cool water, and a soft blanket. Dang curled up beside Nan's bed all night long.

The world outside truly was beautiful, and Dang was glad he had seen it.

But now he understood something even clearer — wherever the people who love you are, that is the best home of all.

In the days that followed, Dang still liked to lie and look out past the fence. But he no longer felt the urge to go out alone.

Sometimes Nan would take him for a walk together, with a leash, with Nan by his side, and a home waiting for him when he returned.

That was so much better.

**Moral:** Adventure is fun, but it's even better when shared with someone you love. Home isn't just a place — it's wherever someone is always waiting for you, and no other warmth in the world compares to that.
