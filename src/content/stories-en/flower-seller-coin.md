Mali woke before sunrise every day to help her mother arrange flowers for the morning market.

Red roses, white jasmine, golden marigolds — each bundle tied neatly with a strip of banana leaf. Mali had done this since she was six. Now she was nine, and her fingers knew the work without thinking.

But this morning was different.

When she lifted her flower basket to move it, she heard a sound.

Clink.

A gold coin lay on the ground where her basket had been sitting.

---

Mali picked it up and held it to the early light. It was real. Heavy. Bright.

She looked around. The market was just waking up — vendors setting out goods, smoke rising from food stalls, a few early customers wandering.

Nobody was watching.

Mali's heart thumped. This coin could buy many things. New sandals, which she badly needed. School supplies. Medicine for her mother's cough.

She slipped it into her pocket.

---

All morning, Mali arranged flowers and called out to customers. But she couldn't stop touching the coin through the cloth of her pocket.

Around midmorning, an older woman came rushing through the market, looking panicked.

"Has anyone seen a gold coin? I dropped it near the flower stalls. It's my daughter's tuition payment — I've saved for it all year!"

Mali froze.

The woman's eyes were red from crying.

Mali's hand closed around the coin.

She could pretend she hadn't heard. She could walk away. Nobody had seen her find it. Nobody would ever know.

But Mali would know.

---

She stepped forward.

"Excuse me," she said quietly. "I think I found it."

She held out the coin.

The woman grabbed it and pressed it to her chest, tears running down her face. "Oh, thank goodness. Thank goodness. Bless you, child. Bless you."

She pressed three smaller coins into Mali's hand before hurrying away.

Mali looked down at the coins. Not gold, but real. Earned honestly.

She went back to her flowers and kept working.

At the end of the day, her mother counted the earnings.

"You sold well today," her mother said.

"Someone lost something and I gave it back," Mali said simply.

Her mother looked at her for a moment, then nodded slowly.

"That's worth more than anything we could sell," she said.

**Lesson:** Honesty is hardest when nobody is watching and the temptation is real. But the peace that comes from doing what's right belongs only to the person who chooses it.
