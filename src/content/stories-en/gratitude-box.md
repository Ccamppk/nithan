May was a girl who loved to complain.

Breakfast wasn't tasty enough. School was too far. Her friends didn't play with her enough. Her room was too small. Nothing was ever quite good enough for May.

One day, her homeroom teacher looked at her and said gently, "May, I have a task for you to do for one week."

May thought it would be homework, but the teacher pulled out a small blue paper box.

"This box is called the Gratitude Box," the teacher said. "Every night before bed, write one small note about something you're grateful for today, fold it up, and put it in the box. It doesn't need to be much — just one thing is enough."

May took the box with a less-than-enthusiastic look. "What should I be grateful for, Teacher?"

"Anything that made you feel even a little bit good today."

That first night, May sat thinking for a long while. Nothing seemed worth being grateful for at all. She almost set the box aside — but then she remembered the weather had been cool that day, and she liked cool weather.

She wrote, "Grateful that it was breezy today," and folded it into the box.

The second night came more easily. She wrote, "Grateful Mom made stir-fried chicken for dinner, my favorite."

The third night: "Grateful the girl who sits next to me smiled at me this morning."

By the fourth night, May noticed she had started looking for good things throughout the whole day, just so she'd have something to write.

On the way to school, she noticed a dragon fruit flower blooming by a fence — pretty. At lunch, she noticed the school food was hotter and tastier than usual. In the evening, she noticed her little brother came to ask if he could look at her homework together, something he never used to do.

By the end of the week, May sat down and opened the box, pulling out each slip of paper to read.

Seven notes. Seven things she had never noticed before.

Her teacher asked her on Monday how it had gone.

"It feels strange, Teacher," May said. "Home is the same, school is the same, but I feel better — even though nothing changed."

The teacher smiled. "That's exactly it. Happiness doesn't come from life changing — it comes from us changing how we look at it."

May looked down at the small blue box in her hands. "Do I have to keep doing this, Teacher?"

"It's not required," the teacher said. "But do you want to?"

May thought for a moment, then nodded.

She kept writing notes and putting them in the box every night for months after that. Some nights she wrote several notes; some nights just one.

And every time life felt heavy or unfair, she would open the box and pull out any note at random to read.

She always felt lighter afterward.

Because inside that box was proof that her life held good things, always there, waiting for her to notice, every single day.

**Moral:** Happiness doesn't come from having everything we want — it comes from seeing the value in what we already have. Try finding one thing to be grateful for each day, and life will change without anything changing at all.
