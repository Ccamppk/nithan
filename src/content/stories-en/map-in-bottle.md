Ton and Pla had been friends since they were little. Their houses stood side by side, they went to the same school, and every evening they loved walking along the canal behind their homes.

That day, Ton spotted something glinting on the bank — a small glass bottle sealed with a wooden stopper, with a folded piece of paper inside.

"Grab it!" Pla said excitedly.

Ton reached down and picked up the bottle, pulled out the stopper, and shook the paper loose. He unfolded it to find an old map drawn in brown ink, with a star symbol in one corner and arrows pointing to different locations.

"Treasure!" Pla shouted.

"Shh, quiet," Ton laughed, just as excited himself.

According to the map, the first spot was a big mango tree by the old road. Both of them knew that tree and ran there in ten minutes.

At the base of the mango tree was an oddly shaped stone. They turned it over and found a small folded note that read: A good person's treasure is refusing to give up. Next, go to the pavilion by the water.

"Aw, not gold," Pla pouted.

"But there's more," Ton said, and ran off ahead.

The waterside pavilion was a bit further away. An arrow pointed to one of the wooden posts. Behind the post was a small metal box tied with old rope. Inside was a note that read: A wise person's treasure is knowing to ask when you don't understand. Next, go to the house with the red roof by the canal.

The two of them ran to the red-roofed house, where an elderly woman sat outside.

"Excuse me, ma'am," Ton walked up. "We're following a treasure map, and it led us here."

The old woman looked at the two of them and smiled. "I knew someone would come."

She disappeared into the house for a moment, then came back out with an old metal tin. She opened it to reveal black-and-white photographs and a folded letter.

"This is the real treasure," the old woman said. "I set up this map ten years ago, hoping some children would find it one day."

Ton picked up the letter and read it aloud.

"The most valuable treasure in the world isn't gold or silver, but a friend willing to journey alongside you, and the memories you build together. No one can ever steal these treasures."

Pla turned to look at Ton, and the two of them burst out laughing together.

"That was very clever of you," Pla said to the old woman.

The old woman laughed. "I'm old, but I still know what matters most."

That evening, Ton and Pla sat by the canal where they'd found the bottle, watching the water flow, talking about everything they'd discovered that day.

No gold, no jewels — but it was one of the best days of their lives.

And that was more than enough.

**Moral:** The most valuable treasures in life aren't objects — they are true memories and friendship. Time spent with people we love is a treasure that never runs out and can never be taken away.
