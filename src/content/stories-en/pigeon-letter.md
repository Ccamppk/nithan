Once upon a time, there was a silver pigeon named Silver who had delivered letters for the village for thirty years.

No matter how hard the rain fell, how strong the wind blew, or how scorching the heat, Silver flew out every morning and returned home having delivered every single letter, without fail.

A boy named Naw had known Silver since he was born. His grandfather was the one who cared for the pigeons, and Naw loved feeding Silver every day.

One day, a great storm arrived. Thunder crashed and lightning flashed, and the wind blew so hard the trees swayed violently.

Naw saw Silver preparing to fly out and ran to his grandfather. "Grandpa, why does Silver have to fly out in a storm? Can't the letters wait?"

Grandfather shook his head. "Ask Silver yourself."

Naw looked at Silver, who was stretching out his wings, and asked softly, "Silver, aren't you afraid of the storm?"

Silver turned to look at Naw with his small red eyes, then flew straight out into the storm.

Naw followed his grandfather back inside the house and waited.

Three hours later, Silver returned, his wings soaked, feathers ruffled — but the pouch tied to his leg was empty. Every letter had been delivered.

Grandfather dried Silver off and told Naw, "Silver doesn't know how important the letters on his leg really are. Sometimes it's news about someone who's ill. Sometimes it's about business. Sometimes it might be a love letter someone has waited three months for. But Silver only knows one thing — once he takes on the task of delivering it, he must deliver it."

"But what if something happened to Silver, Grandpa?"

"That's the risk of keeping a promise," Grandfather said. "A true promise isn't something we keep only when it's convenient — it's something we hold onto even when it's hard."

Naw looked at Silver resting in his cage, and thought of all the promises he himself had made but never kept. He thought of the friend he'd said he'd help but forgot. He thought of his mother, whom he'd promised to help with the dishes.

The next day, he did every single thing he had promised.

Because Naw now understood — a person's word should be as reliable as Silver's wings in the storm.

**Moral:** A promise kept only when convenient isn't a true promise. A trustworthy person is one who follows through on what they said, even when it's difficult or inconvenient, because our words are what others use to judge who we truly are.
