In October, the city of Jaipur in India was alive with the smell of sweets and bursts of color. Every shop was filled with Diwali lamps, marigold garlands, and bags of colored powder for making rangoli patterns on doorsteps.

Nine-year-old Aryan ran through the market with his father, his eyes darting in every direction.

"I want that one!" He pointed to a golden lotus-shaped lamp hanging outside a shop.

"It's expensive," his father said.

"But it's so beautiful, Papa. This year I want our house to be the most beautiful one on the whole street."

His father smiled. "Diwali isn't a competition to see who looks best, Aryan."

"Then what is Diwali, Papa?"

His father stopped walking and looked at his son with a thoughtful expression. "Let's talk at home."

---

That evening the whole family sat in a circle on the living room floor. Grandmother Amma, eighty-one years old, sat in her old wooden chair holding a small clay lamp in her wrinkled hands.

"Diwali begins with the story of Prince Rama," Grandmother said. "After he defeated the demon king Ravana and brought Queen Sita home, the people of his city lit lamps to welcome him back. A million lamps shining together - that was their way of showing joy."

"But why do we light lamps every year, Grandma?" Aryan asked.

"Because every year, darkness still exists," she answered. "And every year, we need to remind ourselves that light is always stronger. No matter how great the darkness, even a single lamp can shine through it."

Aryan looked at the small clay lamp in his grandmother's hands and felt, somehow, that it was more beautiful than any golden lamp in the market.

---

The next day, Aryan helped his mother make laddoo - round sweets made from gram flour and palm sugar. While he kneaded the dough, he glanced out the window at the house next door, where his friend Akash lived.

The house was quieter than usual. No lamps hung outside, no decorations, no preparations.

"Is something wrong with Akash?" Aryan asked.

His mother stopped kneading. "Akash's father has been very ill in hospital. All their money has gone to pay for treatment."

Aryan was quiet for a while, staring down at the dough in his hands.

"Mama," he said, "can we make extra laddoo?"

"Why?"

"I want to take some to Akash."

---

Diwali night came. The whole street blazed with hundreds of lamps. Firecrackers popped at intervals, and the smell of gunpowder mixed with the scent of marigolds strewn along the path.

Aryan's house was beautifully decorated. His mother had made an elaborate rangoli in front of the door. His father had hung lamps along every railing. And Aryan had helped line the steps with little clay lamps, each one glowing.

But before the family sat down together, Aryan picked up the box of laddoo he had set aside and walked next door to knock on Akash's door.

Akash answered looking pale and tired.

"Here," Aryan held out the box. "Mama made too much. We can't eat it all ourselves."

Akash took the box in slightly trembling hands.

"And this too," Aryan said, pulling two clay lamps from his pocket. "Put them outside your house. Tonight every house needs a light."

Akash looked at the lamps, then at his friend. Tears slid down his face before he could stop them. "Thank you."

"Don't thank me," Aryan said. "This is what friends do for each other."

He helped Akash set the lamps outside the door and light them. Two small flames rose in the darkness.

---

When Aryan came back, his father was waiting at the door, watching with an expression Aryan had never seen before.

"I saw what you did," his father said quietly.

"I gave Akash two of our lamps. We still have plenty left."

His father put a hand on his son's shoulder. "When I told you that Diwali isn't a competition to see who looks the most beautiful - do you understand now what I meant?"

Aryan looked out at the lamps glowing all along the street. Then he looked at the two small flames in front of Akash's house.

"Yes, Papa," he said. "The brightest light isn't the golden lamps or the electric lights. It's the light we give to each other."

His father nodded, then led his son inside.

Outside, a thousand lamps glowed across the city of Jaipur. And among all those lights, the two small flames in front of Akash's door shone brightest of all, in Aryan's eyes.
