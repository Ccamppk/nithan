In an old wooden house beside a rice field, a girl named "Ao" lived with her grandmother, whom she loved very much. The house was not grand, but it was full of old things her grandmother had collected throughout her life.

One afternoon after school, Ao ran inside full of curiosity. She noticed the storage room door was not fully closed — a country breeze had pushed it open just enough.

"Grandma isn't home..." Ao murmured to herself, and stepped inside the dim room that smelled faintly of old wood.

In the center of the room sat an old wooden box on a shelf. Ao lifted it down — it was heavier than she expected, with a small key hanging on the side. She turned the key, and the box clicked open.

Inside was an old gold necklace, glimmering softly in the light filtering through the window. There were also black-and-white photographs of people she didn't recognize, and an old folded letter.

"How beautiful..." Ao lifted the necklace. It was light and smooth in her hand. She wanted to try it on just once.

But a voice rose inside her: "It belongs to Grandma. You need to ask first."

Ao froze. Her hand, about to raise the necklace, stopped. She gently placed it back in the box, closed the lid, and returned everything to exactly where she had found it.

That evening, when Grandma returned, Ao sat waiting on the porch. Grandma saw her granddaughter's face and knew immediately there was something she needed to say.

"I went into your storage room, Grandma. I opened the wooden box. I'm sorry," Ao said directly, her voice trembling a little.

Grandma was quiet for a moment, then smiled. "You told me yourself. Without being asked."

"I knew I had done something wrong. So I had to say."

Grandma led Ao inside and brought out the wooden box. She opened it gently so they could look together.

"This is your great-grandmother's necklace," Grandma said, lifting it with care. "When I was young, Great-Grandma told me this necklace would be passed on to the most honest person in the family."

Ao was puzzled. "And who did you give it to?"

Grandma looked at her granddaughter with warm eyes. "Today I know exactly who to give it to."

Grandma slowly placed the necklace around Ao's neck. The thin gold chain rested against the small girl's skin. Ao felt such warmth that her eyes filled with tears.

"I'm not giving it to you because you opened the box," Grandma said. "I'm giving it because you were brave enough to tell the truth. Most people, when they do something wrong, pretend nothing happened."

Ao hugged her grandmother tight. "I won't ever lie to you again."

Grandma stroked her granddaughter's hair gently. "I know."

That night Ao fell asleep feeling lighter than air, as though honesty truly weighed far less than a lie. The gold necklace hung on the hook beside her bed, glinting softly in the moonlight — the best reminder she would ever have.

From that day on, no matter what happened, Ao would think of the wooden box and the gold necklace, and choose to tell the truth, however difficult it might be.

**Moral:** Honesty is not just about not lying — it is about the courage to speak the truth even after making a mistake. The person who can admit when they are wrong is the strongest person of all.
