Once upon a time, deep in a forest no one dared enter, there lived a deer whose fur shone golden like sunlight. Every tree it passed burst into bloom; every stream it drank from ran clearer.

A hunter named Saeng had supported his family by hunting for ten years. But that season, animals had grown scarce, his stomach was empty, and his two children waited at home for food.

The day he found the golden deer's tracks, his heart raced. If he could catch it and sell it, his family could eat well for months.

Saeng followed the tracks across a stream, through a meadow, deep into the forest, until he found the golden deer standing in a beam of light filtering through the leaves.

He raised his bow, drew the string, and aimed.

The golden deer turned and looked at him.

Its golden eyes were deep as a lake — unafraid, unmoving, simply watching.

Saeng held his position for a long moment. His fingers on the bowstring began to tremble.

In the deer's eyes, he saw something he couldn't explain. He saw his children at home. He saw his aging mother. He saw himself as a boy, once running and playing in this very forest.

He lowered his bow.

The golden deer stood watching him a moment longer, then turned and walked slowly, unhurried, deeper into the forest.

Saeng sat down on the forest floor, his heart both heavy and light at the same time.

He walked home empty-handed, not knowing what he would tell his children. But along the way, he came across a huge wild beehive he had never noticed before, golden honey overflowing from every comb.

And beside the hive was a small cloth pouch left by no one he could see, filled with seeds of every kind he had never encountered.

Saeng carried everything home and planted the seeds in his backyard garden. Three weeks later, the whole garden had sprouted, bearing fruit abundant enough to feed his family for a full year.

When his neighbors asked, he shared the seeds with the entire village.

That year, every household prospered.

Saeng never saw the golden deer again. But he always believed that the deer had heard the decision he made in the forest that day.

And the true reward wasn't in the catching — it was in the letting go.

**Moral:** Sometimes restraint and giving other lives a chance is the very act that brings the greatest good into our own lives. True kindness asks for nothing in return, yet it is often repaid all the same.
